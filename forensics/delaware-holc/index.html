The February 8, 1865 Rejection Vote

Context: The Democrat-controlled Delaware General Assembly voted to reject the 13th Amendment, making Delaware the first state in the Union to formally reject abolition.

The 73rd Delaware General Assembly (1865-1866):

  • Legislative session: January 3, 1865 - January 1, 1867
  • Apportionment: 3 senators + 7 representatives per county (3 counties = 9 senators, 21 representatives)
  • Senate Majority: Democratic. Speaker: William Hitch
  • House Majority: Democratic. Speaker: Shephard P. Houston (Sussex County)

Key Details:

  • Governor William Cannon (Republican) recommended approval but was powerless against Democratic majority
  • The legislature declared its "unqualified disapproval" of the amendment
  • Rationale: amendment was "contrary to the principles upon which the government was framed"
  • Governor Cannon died in office March 1, 1865 (three weeks after the vote)
  • Gove Saulsbury (Democrat, Speaker of the Senate) became governor upon Cannon's death
  • Saulsbury continued anti-abolition stance, lamented 13th Amendment ratification in January 1867 address

Named Perpetrators:

  • Gove Saulsbury (Governor 1865-1871): Led opposition to all three Civil Rights amendments
  • Willard Saulsbury Sr. (US Senator, Gove's brother): Called 15th Amendment "an exercise of absolute and tyrannical power"
  • William Hitch (Senate Speaker): Presided over rejection vote
  • Shephard P. Houston (House Speaker, Sussex County): Presided over House rejection
  • Democratic caucus of Delaware General Assembly: Voted unanimously against abolition
ARCHIVAL NOTE: Individual roll call votes are in the Journals of the 73rd Delaware General Assembly at Delaware Public Archives (Dover) and University of Delaware Special Collections. Not digitized online.

1901 Symbolic Ratification

On January 31 and February 6, 1901, the General Assembly finally passed a joint resolution symbolically ratifying the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments... 36 years after the 13th Amendment was ratified without them. Delaware, along with Kentucky and Mississippi, were the last three states to ratify.

Black Codes and Apprenticeship System

After the Civil War, Delaware enacted Black Codes that:

  • Subjected Black people to criminal prosecution for loitering, vagrancy, curfew violations
  • Mandated yearly labor contracts or face arrest
  • Allowed the state to seize Black children and force them into unpaid apprenticeships without parental consent
  • Apprenticeship contracts theoretically provided food, clothing, and "education" but were exploitation
GAMMA NOTE: Delaware's Black Codes were described as "chillingly similar" to antebellum codes... the Resistance Ratchet in action. When M1 (slavery) was blocked, Delaware immediately shifted to M2 (Black Codes + forced apprenticeship).

  • Ethel Louise Belton and six other parents of eight Black high school students in Claymont, DE

  • Chancellor Collins J. Seitz ruled segregation inherently harmful... first clear victory for opponents of segregation in an American court

MECHANISM 8: The Whipping Post (1717-1952)

Red Hannah: Delaware's Whipping Post Targeted Black Bodies (1900-1945)

NEW MECHANISM (not in original scaffold). Delaware's uniquely brutal system deserves standalone treatment.

"Red Hannah"

Delaware maintained the whipping post from 1717 to 1952... the last state in America to abolish corporal punishment. The statute was not removed until 1972.

Scale and Racial Targeting

Period People Whipped % Black Black % of Population Gamma
1900-1945 1,600+ ~70% <20% ~3.5x
  • If women were whipped, "it was Black women only"
  • The pillory was abolished in 1905, but whipping continued 47 more years
  • The last physical whipping post was not removed from public display until July 2020

Academic Documentation

Harvey Young (Johns Hopkins): "Whipping the Black Body in Delaware" documents how public whippings functioned as "a theatre of punishment" that "normalized (and rendered mundane) societal witnessing of violence against people with brown skin."

First recorded public whipping in Delaware: 1656.

New Castle County Workhouse (est. 1901)

  • Prisoners required to work 8 hours/day
  • Trustees could make agreements with "the Levy Court, private individuals, or corporations for use of a convict's labor"
  • This IS a form of convict leasing, administered through the workhouse rather than direct lease
  • Operated a prison farm employing 150+ inmates
  • Wages for jailed workers: $0.25 to $2.00/hour, unchanged for decades
  • Also the site of corporal punishment execution (whipping)
EEDTM NOTE: Delaware did not need Southern-style convict leasing because it had the whipping post as a cheaper disciplinary mechanism and the workhouse as forced labor. The racial targeting (70% Black, <20% population) validates a Gamma of ~3.5x. The workhouse's authority to contract convict labor to private entities IS functionally convict leasing.
